# Research Notes: After the Shock

Notes on research related to the economic shock of AI-driven labor displacement.

---

## The AI Layoff Trap

**Authors:** Brett Hemenway Falk (University of Pennsylvania), Gerry Tsoukalas (Boston University)
**Published:** March 2026
**Paper:** [arxiv.org/abs/2603.20617](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.20617)

### Core Argument

If AI displaces human workers faster than the economy can reabsorb them, it risks eroding the very consumer demand firms depend on. Knowing this is not enough for firms to stop it. In a competitive task-based model, demand externalities trap rational firms in an automation arms race, displacing workers well beyond what is collectively optimal.

### The Trap

- Every CEO automating with AI is making the smartest possible decision for their company individually
- But the lost spending power from displaced workers spreads across the entire economy, so each firm only absorbs a small fraction of the damage it creates
- The result is a race no one can step out of: any company that slows down loses ground to competitors that don't
- More competition and "better" AI amplify the excess
- The resulting loss harms both workers and firm owners

### What Doesn't Work

The authors tested the most common proposed solutions against their model and found that most fail to fix the core incentive driving the cycle:

- Capital income taxes
- Worker equity participation
- Universal basic income (UBI)
- Upskilling programs
- Coasian bargaining
- Wage adjustments and free entry

None of these address the underlying demand externality.

### What Works

Only a **Pigouvian automation tax** — set equal to the uninternalized demand loss per task — implements the cooperative optimum. Its revenue can fund retraining that raises income replacement, shrinking the externality over time and making the tax potentially self-limiting.

### Key Takeaway

Policy should address not only the aftermath of AI labor displacement but also the competitive incentives that drive it. The problem isn't irrational behavior — it's that rational individual choices add up to a bad collective outcome.
